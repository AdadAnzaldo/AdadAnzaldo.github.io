Bio-Inspired Robotics for Agricultural Monitoring

Developed as my Bionic Engineering thesis, this project explores how nature-inspired locomotion, intelligent control systems, and computer vision can be combined into a single autonomous robotic platform.

The resulting hexapod robot was designed to traverse irregular terrain while perceiving and reacting to its environment, demonstrating the potential of bio-inspired robotics for next-generation agricultural inspection and monitoring tasks.

This work combines mechanical design, embedded systems, fuzzy control, computer vision, and autonomous navigation into a fully integrated robotic solution.

Technologies Core Components

The hexapod robot was conceived as a bio-inspired robotic platform capable of traversing irregular terrain while collecting visual information for agricultural inspection tasks.

The locomotion system was developed from the study of real ant gait patterns, allowing the robot to reproduce stable walking behaviors through coordinated control of eighteen degrees of freedom. Kinematic analysis and gait generation algorithms were used to achieve smooth and reliable movement across complex environments.

To support autonomous operation, the platform integrates fuzzy logic control and stereoscopic computer vision. Visual feedback enables the robot to perceive its surroundings and adapt its behavior, while fuzzy inference provides robust decision-making in situations where traditional mathematical models are difficult to define.

The complete system was modeled and validated in ROS and Gazebo before deployment on the physical platform, enabling simulation, testing, and iterative refinement of locomotion and navigation behaviors.

Architecture

High-level architecture showing the interaction between the all modular systems.

The hexapod robot was designed using a modular architecture that combines perception, intelligent control, locomotion planning, and actuation into a unified robotic system.

Visual information acquired through the stereoscopic camera is processed to identify targets and estimate environmental conditions. This information is then used by the fuzzy controller to generate adaptive navigation decisions.

Locomotion commands are translated into coordinated leg trajectories through kinematic models, allowing the robot to maintain stability while traversing uneven terrain. The complete architecture was validated in ROS and Gazebo before deployment on the physical platform.

Testing & Evaluation

The development process involved extensive validation at both the simulation and physical prototype levels to ensure reliable locomotion, navigation, and environmental perception.


Before deployment on the real robot, gait generation algorithms, inverse kinematics, and control strategies were tested in ROS and Gazebo, allowing rapid iteration and risk-free experimentation.


The final platform was evaluated through locomotion tests, target tracking scenarios, and computer vision experiments, demonstrating the feasibility of combining bio-inspired robotics, fuzzy control, and visual feedback for autonomous agricultural inspection.
